Prairie Habitats

Missouri’s prairies are part of the once vast tallgrass prairie ecosystem that historically covered much of the central United States. At one time, Missouri alone boasted more than 15 million acres of prairie, covering roughly one-third of the state. Today, however, less than 65,000 acres remain, which is only about 0.4% of the original landscape. These remaining prairies are considered imperiled ecosystems and are now primarily found in the southwestern region of the state. Protected areas such as Prairie State Park and Taberville Prairie preserve important remnants of this once-dominant habitat and provide opportunities to study and experience native prairie environments.

Despite their reduced size, Missouri prairies support an incredible level of biodiversity. Tall grasses and wildflowers create a complex habitat that provides food and shelter for a wide range of wildlife. Large mammals such as American bison have been reintroduced in some protected areas, helping restore natural ecological processes. Prairies are also especially important for bird species, including meadowlarks, grasshopper sparrows, and other ground-nesting birds that rely on open grasslands for survival. In addition to birds and mammals, prairies are home to hundreds of plant species and countless insects, many of which are highly specialized and cannot survive in other environments.

The importance of Missouri’s prairies extends far beyond their beauty and wildlife. These ecosystems play a critical role in maintaining environmental balance by improving soil health, supporting pollinators, and helping manage water through deep root systems that reduce erosion and increase water absorption. Because so little prairie remains, conservation efforts are essential to protect and restore these habitats for future generations. By preserving prairies, Missouri not only safeguards its natural heritage but also ensures that the ecological benefits of these landscapes continue to support both wildlife and human communities.

Prairie Conservation Efforts in Missouri

Missouri’s prairies have experienced one of the most dramatic declines of any ecosystem in the state, with less than one percent of the original tallgrass prairie remaining. Because of this loss, conservation efforts are critical to protect, restore, and manage these fragile landscapes. Today, a combination of state agencies, conservation groups, and restoration programs work together to preserve prairie habitats and maintain the biodiversity they support.

The Missouri Department of Conservation (MDC) plays a major role in prairie restoration and management through controlled burns, invasive species removal, and habitat protection. In addition, The Nature Conservancy works to preserve native prairie land and restore ecosystems across the state. Their efforts help protect rare plant species and provide habitat for grassland birds and other wildlife.

Other organizations also contribute to prairie conservation in Missouri. The Tallgrass Prairie Center and related prairie initiatives support restoration research and education, helping improve conservation techniques. The Xerces Society focuses on protecting pollinators, which are essential to prairie ecosystems. Together, these groups help restore native grasses, support wildlife populations, and ensure that Missouri’s remaining prairies continue to survive and recover.

Conservation Highlight: Missouri Prairie Foundation

The Missouri Prairie Foundation is a leading organization dedicated specifically to prairie conservation. For over 60 years, it has worked to protect and restore native grasslands through land management, education, and research.

The foundation also supports programs such as Grow Native! and the Missouri Invasive Plant Council, which focus on promoting native plants and controlling invasive species that threaten prairie ecosystems.
